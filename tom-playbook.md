# How to Be Tom on the Mango Sciences Call

*Synthesized from 6 client calls: Einstein, Alliance Bernstein, Inchcape, News UK, Pacific Life, Lumanity*

---

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

Tom listens 60% of the time. When he speaks, he reflects back what he heard to prove he understood it, asks one devastating question, then shuts up again. He never solutionises early. He admits what he doesn't know. He makes the client feel like the expert on their own problem while quietly steering toward where Conquer can deliver. He closes every call with concrete next steps and the phrase "earn our stripes."

---

## TOM'S 7 MOVES (in order of when they happen on a call)

### MOVE 1: The Warm Open
Tom opens every call like he's catching up with a mate, not pitching a prospect.

**What he actually says:**
- "Talk to me, bud. How are you doing?"
- "Hey, how are you? Great to meet you."
- "Yeah, I got you."

**For Mango today:** You've already met these people. Open warm, reference the last call casually. "Good to see everyone again" energy, not "let's get down to business" energy.

---

### MOVE 2: The Credential Drop (light, fast, then move on)
Tom states his title once, matter-of-factly, then immediately pivots to asking about them.

**What he actually says:**
- "My name's Thomas Buckley, I'm the Chief Technical Officer, co-founder here at Conquer AI. But look, it'd probably be useful just to hear a little bit more about where you guys are."

**For Mango today:** You don't need to re-introduce Conquer. Just position yourself: "I'm here to really get into the technical detail today and understand your roadmap so we can figure out where we can be most useful."

---

### MOVE 3: The Diagnostic Question
This is Tom's signature. Before he says anything substantive, he asks where they are. He uses a spectrum framing to normalise all answers.

**What he actually says:**
- "What we like to do to try and understand where we can be the most valuable is just to maybe try and understand more as to where you're currently at in your AI adoption. Are you guys quite new to AI? Are you still trying to work out where to place it? Do you already have some success? We work with clients across the whole spectrum."
- "Do you guys have an intuition for where the parts of this that are probably most primed for AI?"

**For Mango today:** They're presenting their roadmap. Before they start, set the frame: "Before you walk us through the roadmap, it'd be useful to know — of all the use cases you've identified, where do you feel the most urgency? Like which ones are keeping you up at night versus which ones are more aspirational?"

---

### MOVE 4: The Reflective Listen (THIS IS THE KEY MOVE)
After someone explains something, Tom reflects it back in his own words before asking his next question. This is what makes people feel heard and builds trust faster than anything else.

**What he actually says:**
- "OK, so just to maybe say back what I think I heard, just to make sure that I definitely have the right picture. So you're quite advanced with traditional AI — machine learning, neural nets — you've got some success there. But then the more agentic side of things, you're still quite early. Is that fair?"
- "So there's the documented process, and then there's what it actually looks like for you to do. Are you able to maybe demonstrate some of that in reality as opposed to necessarily on paper?"

**For Mango today:** When Hanif presents a use case, don't jump to "here's what we'd do." Instead: "OK so just to make sure I've got this right — you've got ~3M patient records, the harmonisation is currently running through an ontology-based NLP pipeline, and the challenge is that even your structured data is messy enough that agentic pipelines on top of it haven't worked. Is that a fair summary?" Then let him correct or confirm.

---

### MOVE 5: The Non-Determinism Probe
For every use case, Tom applies one devastating question: what happens when the AI gets it wrong?

**What he actually says:**
- "The absolute first question I always ask myself is: what is the consequence of non-determinism?"
- "If we need human-in-the-loop, does that delete the value? Because if HITL reduces the ROI to near-zero, it might not be the right starting point."

**For Mango today:** When they present each use case, ask:
- "What happens if the AI gets this wrong? Is it a minor inconvenience or a patient safety issue?"
- "If we put human-in-the-loop here, does that kill the value proposition?"
- "Can we scope this so the consequence of error is contained?"

This is especially powerful for Mango because they're in oncology. Some use cases (data harmonisation) have recoverable errors. Others (patient outcome prediction) don't.

---

### MOVE 6: The Process Reimagination Reframe
Tom consistently pushes clients to think about the entire process around a use case, not just the AI bit.

**What he actually says:**
- "One thing we've found is you can very narrowly build an AI to do this one thing, but generally you get much more value from trying to reimagine that process based on the changing constraints."
- "If the adoption isn't there, if the utilisation isn't there, the end result is indistinguishable from a technical failure."
- "We break every problem down into the technical and the change management. We put just as much thought into change management as the technical side."

**For Mango today:** When they show you a use case, ask: "What does the end-to-end process look like around this? Because if we agenise this one step but everything upstream and downstream is still manual, the value gets trapped." This echoes what Muna said in the first call — they'll recognise the thinking.

---

### MOVE 7: The Close (concrete next steps, no pressure)
Tom always closes with specific actions, frames them as earned, and positions the whiteboard/alignment doc as the natural next step.

**What he actually says:**
- "Well look, it sounds like we've got some next steps. We'll go through everything, try to understand where the gaps are, and then surface back up to you guys and say — here's the process as we understood it, in our own words."
- "We would love the opportunity to speak to some of your team, understand what they're struggling with, and just see if there's any way that we can help."
- "We need to earn our stripes."

**For Mango today:** After hearing their roadmap: "Look, what I'd love to do is take everything you've shared today, go away, and come back with an alignment document — which is basically us reflecting your problem back to you in our own words, with our view on where we think we can be most valuable and what a solution might look like. If we got something wrong, we iterate. If we got it right, we can talk about what delivery looks like. Does that work for you?"

## TOM'S PHRASES TO STEAL

Use these verbatim. They work because they're natural, not salesy.

| Phrase | When to use it |
|--------|---------------|
| "Just to maybe say back what I think I heard..." | After anyone explains something technical |
| "What's the consequence of non-determinism here?" | When evaluating any use case |
| "We look at this with very naive eyes" | When starting to explore their process |
| "The art of the possible" | When they don't know exactly what they want |
| "Winning the battle but losing the war" | When discussing adoption/change management |
| "Indistinguishable from a technical failure" | When making the case for change management focus |
| "We need to earn our stripes" | When discussing trust and at-risk delivery |
| "Not so that we can reduce headcount, but so we can grow without the associated headcount increase" | When they worry about AI replacing people |
| "How do we set your guys up to own and operate this in the longer term" | When discussing IP ownership and independence |
| "Maybe we got that wrong — let's iterate on that" | When presenting any understanding back |
| "We do all of discovery at our risk, for free" | When discussing commercial approach |
| "Far more examples we can't talk about because of NDAs than the ones we can" | When referencing clinical trial experience |

## TOM'S ANTI-PATTERNS (things he NEVER does)

1. **Never solutionises in a first or second meeting.** He explicitly said: "To be clear, we don't know anything about your business."
2. **Never uses jargon to impress.** He explains "fine-tuned small language model" in functional terms, never drops acronyms to flex.
3. **Never claims wisdom he didn't earn.** "I'd like to say this was from wisdom, but honestly it was just from the pain of doing it wrong."
4. **Never pushes for a sale.** Uses "we'd love the opportunity" not "here's what we should do next."
5. **Never positions Conquer as the expert dispensing wisdom.** Always frames it as: "We've seen a lot of people solve this — here's what worked best."
6. **Never talks about Conquer for more than 2 minutes** without pivoting back to asking about them.

## THE MANGO-SPECIFIC GAME PLAN

**Minutes 0-5: Warm up**
- "Good to see everyone again. I'm really looking forward to getting into the detail today."
- Let Mohit or Hanif set the scene.

**Minutes 5-30: They present their roadmap**
- Listen. Take notes. Ask clarifying questions as they go.
- After each use case, do the reflective listen: "Just to make sure I've got this right..."
- Then one probe: "What happens if the AI gets this wrong here?"

**Minutes 30-45: You probe deeper**
- "Walk me through what the data actually looks like today — what's harmonised, what's still raw?"
- "When you say agentic readiness — what does that state look like to you? What needs to be true about the data?"
- "The patient similarity work — where do you see the ceiling with the traditional approach?"
- "Your annotation team — where do they spend the most time? What's the most thankless part of their day?"

**Minutes 45-55: Connect and frame**
- Reference relevant experience: "We've got clients in the clinical trial space — can't name them, can't describe the work, but the domain is one we understand."
- If data harmonisation comes up as the priority: "That feels like the foundation everything else depends on. And if we can get that right, it unlocks everything downstream."
- Drop the flywheel line back at them: "Mohit, you said something last time that stuck with me — the ability to build applications is more the goal than building any one application. That's exactly how we think about it."

**Minutes 55-60: Close with next steps**
- "Here's what I'd love to do. Let us take everything you've shared today, go away, and come back with what we call an alignment document. It's basically us playing back your problem in our own words — here's what we understood, here's where we think we can help, here's what a solution might look like. If we got something wrong, we iterate. If it lands, we can talk about timelines and costs. Sound good?"

## THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Tom's secret isn't what he says. It's that he makes the other person feel like the smartest person in the room while quietly steering the conversation toward where Conquer can deliver. He does this through three things:

1. **Reflecting back** — proves he listened
2. **Asking the hard question** — proves he's thinking
3. **Admitting what he doesn't know** — proves he's honest

Do those three things and you'll be fine.
