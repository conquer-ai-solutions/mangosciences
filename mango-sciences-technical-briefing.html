Organisation Context

A cancer data platform
with massive ambitions.

Mango Sciences sits at the intersection of oncology data, value-based drug financing, and clinical trials. They're not a typical health-tech startup — they have real data, real revenue mechanisms, and a clear need for agentic AI.

What Mango Sciences actually does

Cancer data platform with ~2.7 million lines of phenotypic clinical data, primarily acquired from India. They operate across two core use cases: (1) a value-based financing mechanism for cancer drugs where patients get a money-back guarantee based on health outcomes, and (2) clinical trials via an invested CRO partner, with their first two trials coming up.

The data challenge — this is their obsession

Data harmonisation is the central technical problem. Almost 3 million patients worth of extremely messy EHR records — even the structured data is messy. They're currently using ontology-based NLP approaches (inspired by Palantir's ontology-first philosophy) and are "wading into GenAI" cautiously.

They're about to go multimodal: genomics and radiology/pathology data are coming. The harmonisation challenge is only going to get harder.

Agentic readiness — their own framing

Hanif's team is focused on getting their data platform into "agentic readiness" — their term, not ours. They understand that letting loose agents on messy data doesn't work (they've tried). This is a sophisticated buyer who knows what they need but hasn't cracked the execution.

The flywheel ambition

Our Approach

Listen first.
Then whiteboard.

Today is about understanding, not proposing. Let Hanif and Gavin present. Probe where it matters. Leave with enough context to produce an alignment doc or whiteboard as the next step.

How this meeting should flow

Phase 1 — Mango presents (30 min): Hanif and Gavin walk through the product roadmap, agentic AI use cases, and lessons from past attempts. Our job: listen, take notes, ask clarifying questions.

Phase 2 — We probe (15 min): For each use case, apply the non-determinism framework. Understand the full process around each opportunity. Push for measurable success criteria.

Phase 3 — Next steps (15 min): Propose the whiteboard as the next step. Offer to produce an alignment doc that reflects our understanding back to them. This is the Conquer way — earn the right to propose by demonstrating understanding first.

For each use case they present:

— What happens when the AI gets it wrong? What's the consequence of non-determinism here?

— What does the end-to-end process look like today? Where does this use case sit within it?

— What would success look like in measurable terms? What KPI moves?

— Have you tried anything here before? What worked, what didn't, and why?

For Hanif (data platform):

— Walk us through the current data architecture — what sits where, what's harmonised, what's still raw?

— When you say "agentic readiness" — what does that state look like to you? What needs to be true about the data before you'd trust an agent with it?

— How does the ontology-based NLP pipeline work today? Where does it break down?

For Gavin (data science):

— The patient similarity work — what models are you using, and where do you see the ceiling with the traditional approach?

— As multimodal data comes in (genomics, radiology), how does that change your modelling approach?

— What does the annotation team's workflow look like? Where do they spend the most time?

Tom's headline framework from the first call, and the single most important lens for evaluating Mango's use cases:

"The absolute first question I always ask myself is: what is the consequence of non-determinism?"

For a given input, a language model can't guarantee a specific output. So for each use case:

1. What happens if the AI is wrong? Is it a minor inconvenience or a patient safety issue?

2. If we need human-in-the-loop, does that delete the value? If HITL reduces the ROI to near-zero, it might not be the right starting point.

3. Can we scope the use case so the consequence of error is contained? Data harmonisation errors can be caught and corrected. Clinical outcome predictions that are wrong can have severe consequences.

This framework will help us rank their use cases by viability, not just desirability.

13 technical scenarios drawn from 12 real Conquer engagements — Playtech, Lumanity, News UK, Einstein, Suvoda, YBS, Azets and more. Click to expand. Use verbatim or adapt.

Principle — from AB & Einstein

Tech agnostic, client-first. Never lead with a stack. Ask about theirs first, then adapt. The spirit is ownership transfer — whatever we build runs in their cloud, their source code, their IP.

Say this
"Before we get into what we'd recommend, can you walk us through what you're currently running on? Cloud provider, primary languages, what your data layer looks like, what models you've experimented with?"
Then follow with
"The spirit of how we work is — how do we set your team up to own and operate this in the longer term. Whatever we build will run in your cloud environment, it's your source code, your IP."

If they push on what Conquer prefers: "We tend to reach for Python, we're model-agnostic — we'll use Claude, GPT, open-source models, whatever fits the use case. For data-heavy work like yours, we'd benchmark extraction accuracy for your specific data types early."

From Azets — structuring unstructured data at scale

100K+ SME clients of fragmented data. Staged approach: auto-validation → pre-flight AI checks → human-in-the-loop → self-service automation.

From Suvoda — accuracy cascade in healthcare

Static/known cases: 99.9% · Semi-novel: 71.6% · Truly novel: 41.4%. Accuracy degrades dramatically as novelty increases. Mango's agentic pipelines failed because they threw agents at the hardest cases first.

From Lumanity — biopharma data processing

Knowledge FRaiME ingested ~50 complex biopharma docs with citation-backed answers. Staged confidence pipeline even in pharma: deterministic rules → AI-assisted → human review.

Say this
"That accuracy cascade is something we've seen across multiple healthcare engagements. When you throw agents at truly novel data, accuracy drops off a cliff — we've seen it go from 99% on known patterns to 40% on novel ones. So what we've found works is a tiered approach: start with the data you can validate deterministically, use agents to accelerate the middle tier where you have partial structure, and keep humans on the genuinely novel cases. Over time, as the agent sees more confirmed examples, that middle tier expands and the human-only tier shrinks."
Then probe
"The ontology-first approach you're taking is actually the right foundation — you need the semantic framework before agents can reason about the data. The question is: where in your current pipeline does the ontology break down? Is it at entity extraction, at relationship mapping, or at harmonisation across different source formats?"
From Playtech — the three-layer model

Orchestration layer — how agents are deployed, coordinated, lifecycle managed. Services layer — shared capabilities like memory, data access, model routing. Governance layer — permissions, logging, cost controls, release management.

From News UK — deterministic / probabilistic split

Within each layer, split components into deterministic (must be bulletproof) and probabilistic (accept non-determinism, contain it with validation gates).

From Playtech — the lifecycle problem

"When you've got 50 business units building 10 agents each, all with different life cycles, all needing version control — within 3 months you'll have 5000 agents." Mango won't have 5000, but they'll have multiple agents across harmonisation, annotation, similarity, trials, and multimodal. The lifecycle question matters early.

Say this
"The way we think about it — and we've built this for other enterprise clients — is three layers. First, the orchestration layer: how do your agents get deployed, versioned, coordinated? For you that's the harmonisation pipeline, the annotation workflows, the clinical trial processing. Second, the services layer: shared capabilities that every agent needs — access to your patient data, your ontology, your model routing, your cost tracking. Third, the governance layer: who can deploy what, what gets logged, how do you audit what the AI did and why."
Then add the deterministic/probabilistic frame
"Within each layer, there's a split between deterministic and probabilistic components. Your data pipeline, your ontology rules, your validation checks — those need to be bulletproof. The LLM calls sit inside the deterministic framework with validation gates around them. So an agent can attempt to harmonise a record, but a validation step checks it against your ontology before it's committed. If confidence is below threshold, it routes to a human annotator."

Tie it to Mohit's flywheel: "This is exactly the flywheel you described — you're not building one application, you're building the platform that makes every subsequent application faster to create, cheaper to run, and safer to operate."

From Einstein — healthcare data sovereignty

"All the data stays in your estate, none of your data ever leaves your estate." Build in-house, integrate into their cloud. Their IP, their source code.

From Lumanity — enterprise LLM governance

30–70 users needed: RBAC (role-based access), environment segmentation (sandbox/dev/prod), privacy controls (prevent accidental upload of sensitive data), audit trails (compliance logging of every AI interaction).

From Playtech — AI security testing

Four threat vectors: prompt injection, data poisoning, hallucination, content safety. For oncology, all four matter intensely — a hallucinated drug interaction is dangerous.

Say this
"With patient data — especially oncology data at this scale — we'd never propose an architecture where data leaves your environment. Everything we build runs in your cloud, your VPC. The models we use for extraction and harmonisation would either be deployed within your infrastructure, or we'd use API calls where only the minimum necessary context is sent — and we'd work with your compliance team to define exactly what that boundary looks like."
On sovereignty
"We've worked with healthcare providers where data sovereignty meant certain data couldn't cross borders at all. Our approach: the AI runs where the data lives, not the other way around. If your Indian patient data needs to stay in India, we build the pipeline to run there."
On governance
"On the governance side — designed into the platform from day one, not bolted on later — you need RBAC so not every user sees every patient record, environment segmentation so dev work never touches production data, audit trails so you can show a regulator exactly what the AI did with any given record, and safety rails around hallucination and content accuracy."
From Suvoda + Lumanity

Fine-tuned small models beat general-purpose large models for extraction tasks. Lumanity chose Claude for long-context and citation capabilities. Right model for each task, not one model for everything.

Say this
"Model selection is something we'd benchmark early in delivery, not assume upfront. For data harmonisation, we'd likely test: a frontier model like Claude for complex reasoning — understanding clinical context, resolving ambiguous records — a fine-tuned smaller model for high-volume extraction where you need consistency and lower cost, and your existing NLP pipeline for the deterministic parts that already work."
On extraction specifically
"One thing we've learned in healthcare: for extraction tasks, a fine-tuned small model often outperforms a general-purpose large model. We had a case where we fine-tuned a model specifically for document extraction and it ended up being more accurate than the humans doing it manually. The key was: we could trace every extracted value back to a citation in the source document, so the reviewer could validate in seconds."

For ontology work: "Mapping messy data to structured cancer ontologies — that's where frontier models earn their keep. They handle the ambiguity, abbreviations, inconsistent terminology across hospitals and countries. But you'd want a smaller, cheaper model for high-volume stuff like dedup and format normalisation."

Gavin has done significant work on patient similarity using traditional ML. He'll be protective of this work. Frame agents as complementary, not replacement.

Say this
"The patient similarity work is really interesting because it's a perfect example of where traditional models and agentic AI complement each other. Your traditional similarity models give you the mathematical rigour for cohort building. What agents can add is the ability to pull in unstructured context — clinical notes, imaging reports, genomic data — that your current models can't easily consume. So the agent enriches the input to your similarity engine rather than replacing it."
Then probe
"The question for us would be: what data is your similarity model currently blind to that a clinician would consider relevant? That gap is usually where agents add the most value."
From Suvoda — clinical trial AI

99.9% accuracy on known patterns, drops to 41.4% on novel protocols. Hallucination is the critical risk in regulated domains. Human-in-the-loop validation is non-negotiable. Fine-tuning helps but doesn't solve truly novel cases.

Say this
"We've got several clients in the clinical trial space — the NDAs mean we can't say their name, let alone what we built. But the domain is one we understand deeply. The core challenge is always the same: you need AI that's accurate enough to trust with patient-affecting decisions, but honest enough to flag when it doesn't know. In clinical, a confident wrong answer is worse than no answer at all."
Then probe their trials
"For your first two trials — which parts of the workflow are administrative burden versus clinical judgement? Site selection, patient matching, regulatory document processing — those are high-volume, pattern-heavy tasks where agents can accelerate. Protocol design and safety monitoring — those need human judgement with AI support, not AI autonomy."
Say this
"Multimodal is where things get really interesting and really hard at the same time. Each modality has its own noise characteristics, its own standards — or lack of standards — and its own regulatory requirements."
The sequencing advice
"Our view: don't try to build a unified multimodal pipeline from day one. Get the text and structured data harmonisation rock-solid first — that's your foundation. Then add genomic data as a second modality, because it's structured even if complex. Imaging comes last because it requires the most specialised models and the validation is hardest."

Tie to platform: "The flywheel Mohit described — this is exactly how you get there. Build the platform for text harmonisation, prove the pattern works, then the same platform architecture extends to new modalities. Each one is a new application on the same three-layer foundation."

Principle: buy infrastructure, build intelligence. Standard orchestration, compute, storage — but the AI components specific to your domain get built bespoke. "If there's going to be custom code, we're going to own that custom code."

From Playtech — the security gauntlet

Adam Mitchell deliberately probed: "How do I protect against prompt injection? How do I know data hasn't been poisoned? How do I know my system doesn't hallucinate? How do I know it doesn't give a stupid answer that's going to get me sued?" Four threats: prompt injection, data poisoning, hallucination, harmful/inaccurate content.

From Suvoda — hallucination in clinical

Novel test cases: 41.4% accuracy. The remaining 58.6% wasn't all wrong — some was plausible-sounding but ungrounded. That's more dangerous than obviously wrong.

Say this
"In oncology, AI security isn't a nice-to-have — it's foundational. We think about four threat vectors: prompt injection — can someone embed malicious instructions in the data the agent is processing? Data poisoning — if the training data or reference ontology has errors, the agent inherits them. Hallucination — the agent generates something that looks clinically plausible but isn't grounded in your actual data. And content safety — making sure the output meets clinical and regulatory standards."
Mitigations
"For each, the mitigation is different. Prompt injection is an architecture problem — you sanitise inputs. Data poisoning is a data quality problem — your ontology and validation pipeline are the defence. Hallucination is solved through citations — every output should trace back to a source record. And content safety is governance — guardrails around what the agent can and can't output."

For oncology specifically: "If the agent can't cite a specific record or ontology entry for its output, it should flag uncertainty rather than fabricate an answer."

From Playtech — "50 companies in a trench coat"

"We don't have almost any common IT stack across all of these units. 80 development teams — everyone is a completely different git solution, build server, programming language, target audience, technology stack." Solution: platform above the fragmentation — central enablement with decentralised execution.

Say this
"We've dealt with this exact pattern — a client with 80 different development teams, completely different tech stacks, grown through acquisition. The answer isn't forcing everyone onto one standard. It's building a platform layer that sits above the fragmentation. You define the target data model — your harmonised ontology — and then build adapters for each source format. The platform handles the routing, transformation, and quality checks. Each new data source is a new adapter, not a new architecture."
Apply to Mango
"For you, that means: each hospital, each country, each EHR system gets an ingestion adapter. The adapters do the initial normalisation. Then the harmonisation agents work on partially-normalised data against your ontology. The annotation team handles what the agents can't resolve. Same platform, different inputs."
